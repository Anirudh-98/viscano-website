## The Brief That Never Works

"Make it look modern. Clean. Premium. A bit like Apple but also approachable."

Every designer has received some version of this brief. And every designer knows the problem: it describes a feeling, not a direction. It's impossible to scope, impossible to evaluate, and — inevitably — the source of endless revision cycles.

The antidote isn't a longer mood board. It's a document that treats design as what it actually is: a problem-solving discipline with measurable outcomes.

## What a PRD Does for Design

A Product Requirements Document (PRD) was originally a software concept — a document that describes what a product should do, who should use it, and how success will be measured. At Viscano, we've adapted this format as our standard project intake document.

A good PRD for a design project answers:

1. **What are we building?** — In one sentence, without jargon
2. **Who is it for?** — Not a demographic, a specific person
3. **What problem does it solve for them?**
4. **What are the constraints?** — Budget, timeline, technical, legal
5. **What does success look like?** — Measurable outcomes
6. **What is out of scope?** — Equally important

## Why Constraints Are a Design Superpower

Designers without constraints make work that's beautiful but arbitrary. Constraints — a 4-week timeline, a ₹80K budget, a mobile-first requirement, an existing design system to match — force creative decisions that are grounded, defensible, and ultimately more durable.

The PRD surfaces constraints early. Before we design a single screen, we know exactly what we can and cannot do. This makes the design process faster and the output more suitable to its context.

## How We Use It at Viscano

When a client engages Viscano, our first deliverable is a Scoped Proposal — and that proposal is built from a PRD, not from a service menu. We ask clients to share a brief, a Notion document, a voice note, or even a 30-minute conversation. We then structure that into a PRD format and send it back for alignment before any work begins.

The PRD becomes the source of truth for the project: what is included, what isn't, when it's due, and what "done" means. It eliminates the two most common project failure modes: scope creep and misaligned expectations.

## You Don't Need a Formal PRD

You don't need a perfectly formatted document. A detailed email, a Notion page, or a clear conversation works. What matters is that we align on:

- What we're building
- Who it's for
- What success looks like
- What the constraints are

Everything else flows from there.
