A practical, battle-tested framework for rebranding without alienating the customers who already love you.

## Why Rebrands Fail

Most rebrands fail not because the new brand is bad — but because the rollout is bad. The company announces a new logo, customers who love the existing brand feel confused or betrayed, and the PR fallout overshadows whatever strategic clarity the new brand was meant to deliver.

The classic case: Gap's 2010 logo change. A perfectly reasonable modern redesign, announced without context, walked back within a week under customer pressure. The lesson wasn't "don't rebrand" — it was "don't rebrand without your audience."

## When to Rebrand

Rebranding is justified when:

- **Your market position has shifted** — You've moved up-market, expanded geographically, or pivoted to a new category
- **Your audience has changed** — The people who buy from you now are different from the people you originally designed for
- **The brand feels misaligned with the product** — What you offer has evolved but the brand still communicates the old version
- **You need to unify** — Post-acquisition, post-merger, or after a product portfolio expansion

Rebranding is not justified because you're bored, or because a competitor changed their logo, or because a new CMO wants to put their stamp on things.

## The Audience Inventory

Before redesigning anything, do an audience inventory. Ask:

1. **What do our existing customers love about the current brand?** This is what you must preserve.
2. **What does our current brand communicate that we want to move away from?** This is what you're changing.
3. **What associations do we want the new brand to create?** This is the direction.
4. **Who is the new audience we're trying to reach?** This informs the visual direction.

The overlap between #1 and #3 is your rebrand brief.

## The Continuity Principle

The best rebrands feel like evolution, not revolution — even when the visual change is significant. Continuity can be maintained through:

- **Colour continuity** — Keep the primary colour family, even if the specific hues shift
- **Typography continuity** — Evolve the type style rather than replacing it entirely
- **Tone of voice continuity** — The brand should still sound like itself, even if it sounds more confident
- **Transition period** — Run the old and new brand in parallel for a defined period before hard cutover

## The Announcement Strategy

How you announce a rebrand is as important as the rebrand itself. The announcement should:

1. **Explain the why** — What changed that made this necessary? Customers want context, not just a new logo.
2. **Honour the history** — Acknowledge what the old brand meant and why it served you for as long as it did.
3. **Demonstrate the new** — Show the rebrand in context. Not just the logo — the website, the packaging, the social feed.
4. **Invite reaction** — Give customers a way to respond and acknowledge their response publicly.
