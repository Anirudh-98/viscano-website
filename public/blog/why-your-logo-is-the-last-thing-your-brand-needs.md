# Why Your Logo Is the Last Thing Your Brand Needs

Most founders rush to get a logo. They spend weeks debating colours and typefaces before they've answered a single question about who they're building for. Here is why that's backwards — and the right order to build a brand that lasts.

## The Logo Is a Symbol, Not a Strategy

A logo is the visual shorthand for everything your brand already means to people. When you see the Nike swoosh, you don't think "that's a nice checkmark" — you think of athletic performance, Michael Jordan, decades of "Just Do It." The swoosh means something because Nike built meaning behind it first.

When founders lead with logo design, they're asking the designer to invent meaning from nothing. The result is usually a mark that looks fine but says nothing — and a brand that never quite coheres.

## What Comes Before the Logo

Before a logo, a brand needs:

**1. A positioning statement**  
Who are you for? What do you do for them that no one else does as well? Why should they believe you?

**2. A point of view**  
What does your brand believe about the world? What is the tension in your market that your brand resolves? This is the source of all good brand voice.

**3. A name**  
Your name is the most important piece of brand real estate you have. It should be memorable, pronounceable, and available as a domain.

**4. A voice**  
How does your brand write? Formal or casual? Technical or plain? The voice should feel consistent whether it's in a pitch deck, a tweet, or a terms of service page.

**5. An audience**  
Not a demographic — a real person. Who is the most important person you're trying to reach? What do they read? What do they fear? What do they want?

## Then — and Only Then — the Logo

Once you have the above, a logo designer has something to work with. They can design a mark that expresses your positioning, reflects your voice, and appeals to your specific audience.

The best logos aren't designed — they're derived. They emerge from a clear brand strategy the way a conclusion emerges from good evidence.

## The Cost of Getting It Wrong

When you build backwards — logo first, strategy later — you end up rebranding. And rebranding is expensive. Not just in money, but in the audience confusion that comes with it.

The brands that never need to rebrand are the ones that answered the hard strategic questions first. They built a mark that meant something from day one, because they knew exactly what they wanted it to mean.
